This note reads recent Eurostat and ECB material through a generational lens. The key question is not whether institutions can repeat the language of stability, but whether younger households experience that stability as fair, legible and materially useful.

Youth unemployment, housing access and transport or energy costs shape perceptions more directly than abstract policy credibility. A generation raised during recurring shocks is likely to evaluate macroeconomics through concrete entry barriers rather than through textbook indicators alone.

That perspective strengthens the associated essay's claim that Generation Alpha will inherit a practical, distributional view of macroeconomic policy rather than a purely ideological one.
